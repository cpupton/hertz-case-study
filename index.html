Overview

The Problem

Hertz's checkout had known UX problems, had gone largely without active design iteration, and was falling behind competitors on clarity and user trust. The goal was straightforward but significant: increase conversion, reduce friction, and drive revenue across both US and global markets. Conversion was measured across two primary cohorts — members and non-members — and across responsive web layouts. The gap was significant: members converted at 30–40%, while non-members sat at just 5–10%. The gap itself was the diagnosis. Members are high-intent users — they've already formed a relationship with the brand, they know what they're getting, and they move through checkout with confidence. Non-members arrive with none of that context. Every ambiguous pricing line, every unanswered question about what's included, every moment of uncertainty about fees at pickup is a reason to abandon. The checkout was designed as if all users arrived with the same level of trust. They don't. The design challenge wasn't to fix a broken flow — it was to rebuild the checkout around the skeptic: a first-time user who needed trust signals, clear value propositions, and transparent pricing to feel confident enough to book.

I was brought in as the product designer to own this problem end to end — from identifying what to test, to writing A/B hypotheses, designing variants, and working within a set of meaningful constraints that shaped every decision.

"A 30-point conversion gap between members and non-members isn't a loyalty problem — it's a trust problem. Members already believed in the brand. Non-members needed the checkout to earn that trust in real time, and it wasn't designed to do that."

Constraints

Designing Within Real-World Guardrails

This wasn't a greenfield redesign. Every hypothesis had to be developed and tested within a set of constraints that made the design challenge genuinely complex:

  • European Commission and legal requirements — EU regulations mandated specific pricing disclosures before a user could complete a booking. What fees are collected at reservation vs. pickup, what is included in the quoted price, and what must be visible to the user before they tap "Book Now" — these weren't suggestions, they were compliance requirements.
  • Pricing transparency obligations — Users had to be clearly informed about the breakdown of charges, with no ambiguity between what's paid now and what's due at the counter. This created real tension with conversion-focused design, where friction is typically minimized.
  • Consent and disclosure visibility — Legal copy and checkbox consent had to appear in specific locations relative to the CTA, which constrained the layout options available for key conversion elements.
  • Multi-brand rollout consideration — While the primary design and testing work was conducted on Hertz.com, successful test variants were subsequently rolled out to Thrifty and Dollar. This meant solutions needed to be structurally sound enough to translate across brand identities without relying on Hertz-specific styling.
  • US checkout as benchmark, not template — The US experience was a reference point for conversion performance, but couldn't be directly replicated due to the legal and regulatory differences between markets.

Rather than treating compliance requirements as obstacles to work around, I used them as a forcing function — if we had to surface pricing information prominently anyway, could we do it in a way that also built user confidence?

Process

How I Approached the Work

The work unfolded in phases, moving from audit and framing through to iterative testing. Hypotheses weren't generated randomly — each one came from a specific observed failure mode in the checkout experience.

Phase 01

Audit & Competitive Benchmarking

I began with a UX audit of the existing Hertz checkout, mapping the full flow against the five core problem areas: pricing transparency, trust signals, value propositions, section hierarchy, and total pricing visibility. I then benchmarked the experience against key competitors and the broader industry standard to understand where the gap was structural vs. cosmetic.

Phase 02

Hypothesis Development

I wrote all A/B hypotheses myself, grounding each one in a specific friction point identified in the audit. Five tests were executed during my time at Hertz, three of which proved measurable conversion lift. A further 8–12 concepts were fully hypothesized and designed — ready to run — when I left the role. The pipeline was built; the testing program had real momentum behind it.

Phase 03

Friction Reduction & Form UX

Conversion was always the headline metric, but friction was the underlying mechanism. A user who hits a confusing form field, an unclear error state, or an ambiguous input requirement doesn't necessarily abandon immediately — but the accumulated friction erodes confidence. Through the checkout audit I identified a significant number of form issues specific to the non-member path, where users are completing fields for the first time without the autofill advantages members benefit from. To address this I built a functional prototype demonstrating correct form field validation, error handling, and interaction behaviors — making the case for improvements to the development team with working, testable code rather than static annotations.

Phase 04

Design & Iteration

Variants were designed to test one meaningful change at a time where possible — isolating variables to generate clean learnings. Early tests focused on the areas I expected would have the most leverage: trust signals, protection module clarity, and value proposition framing. Results were mixed in the early phase, which shaped the direction of subsequent hypotheses.

Phase 05

Analysis & Pivot

When early tests returned flat results, I reframed the question. The problem wasn't that users didn't understand the product — it was that they couldn't verify the total cost before committing. The design wasn't failing to persuade; it was failing to reassure. That insight shifted the focus of subsequent tests toward persistent pricing visibility rather than persuasion mechanics.

Design in Context

Mobile Sticky Footer — A/B Test Variant

On a long-scroll mobile checkout, the total price was frequently out of view when users reached the booking CTA. The sticky footer variant pinned pricing and the primary action to the bottom of the screen throughout the entire checkout flow.

Default State

The sticky footer at rest — total price, payment timing, and the CTA always visible at the bottom of the viewport before the user begins scrolling.

On-Scroll State

As the user scrolls, the footer minimizes, keeping the price and CTA visible and accessible. Pricing confidence never leaves the user's peripheral view.

Key Findings

What Actually Moved the Needle

After a period of flat results from earlier test concepts, three specific design changes produced meaningful conversion lift. The common thread across all three: make the total cost visible and verifiable before asking for commitment.

The Three Changes That Worked

Persistent pricing visibility drove conversion where persuasion alone hadn't.

  • 1 Pricing anchored below legal copy and consent checkbox. Moving the total price to sit immediately beneath the required legal disclosure and consent checkbox — rather than above it or in a separate summary block — meant users saw the final cost in direct proximity to the commitment gesture. The consent requirement, previously a conversion barrier, became a natural pricing confirmation moment.
  • 2 CTA redesigned in Hertz yellow with persistent "Pay Now / Pay at Pickup" pricing. Changing the call-to-action button to Hertz's brand yellow gave it appropriate visual prominence, while embedding both payment option totals directly in or adjacent to the CTA eliminated the need for users to scroll back up to verify pricing before booking. Price and action became a single, confident unit.
  • 3 Sticky footer for mobile web with always-visible pricing. On a long-scroll mobile checkout, the total price was frequently out of view when users reached the booking CTA. A sticky footer pinned the price summary and primary action to the bottom of the screen throughout the checkout — removing a key moment of uncertainty. This test produced measurable lift: roughly +6% for members and +2% for non-members.
